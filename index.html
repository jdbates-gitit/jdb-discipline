Tao Te Ching · Legge translation
Chapter 64

That which is at rest is easily kept hold of; before a thing has given indications of its presence, it is easy to take measures against it; that which is brittle is easily broken; that which is very small is easily dispersed. Action should be taken before a thing has made its appearance; order should be secured before disorder has begun. The tree which fills the arms grew from the tiniest sprout; the tower of nine storeys rose from a (small) heap of earth; the journey of a thousand li commenced with a single step. He who acts (with an ulterior purpose) does harm; he who takes hold of a thing (in the same way) loses his hold. The sage does not act (so), and therefore does no harm; he does not lay hold (so), and therefore does not lose his hold. (But) people in their conduct of affairs are constantly ruining them when they are on the eve of success. If they were careful at the end, as (they should be) at the beginning, they would not so ruin them. Therefore the sage desires what (other men) do not desire, and does not prize things difficult to get; he learns what (other men) do not learn, and turns back to what the multitude of men have passed by. Thus he helps the natural development of all things, and does not dare to act (with an ulterior purpose of his own).

What it's pointing at

This chapter teaches that prevention is far wiser than cure, and that grasping undermines what we seek to hold. A problem is easiest to address before it begins; a tree grows from a single seed; success is lost by those who abandon care at the finish line. The sage acts without forcing, desires what others overlook, and trusts the natural unfolding of things rather than imposing will upon them.

Read against today

We live in a culture of reaction—responding to crises rather than preventing them, fighting symptoms rather than addressing root causes. Our public life churns with grasping: for certainty, for victory, for the next outrage to fuel our righteousness. The chapter's quiet observation cuts deep: we ruin things "on the eve of success" by tightening our grip, by acting from ulterior purpose rather than simple presence. In a world of endless stimulation and conflict, the sage's way—learning what others overlook, valuing what seems small—feels almost subversive.

To carry today

Today, notice what you are trying to control or force into being. Before the day pulls you forward, pause: where can you simply tend rather than grasp? Where can you trust the smallest right action over the dramatic gesture? Carry this through the afternoon: the thing already underway needs less force, not more.
